Trips for seniors: My $2.50 weekend adventure in NSW


﻿I recently received my senior’s card and wanted to give it a run for its money. So my wife and I decided we would getaway for a few days, but where to? 


Well we thought the best place to start would be somewhere we knew accepted the senior’s card, so we went to our local train station and found out just how cheap this getaway would be.


We found out that for $2.50 each we could book a PET (pensioner excursion ticket) to most places in NSW, the added bonus being if we returned in 12 hours we could use the same ticket. So we decided to go and visit some friends and family in Dubbo. We were unsure if we would use our seniors card to receive discount on local transport or if we would hire a car and use our card to receive a discount on car hire.


We opted for car hire, as some of our family lived further away from public transport and were pleasantly surprised to learn would again receive a discount. We decided to add a visit to the zoo in with our trip and received a 30% discount with our card.


Throughout our trip we received discounts on food and coffee at Gloria jeans, Donut King and various pubs and restauruants. We enjoyed a cheap night at the movies with friends, as well as a discount on our medication, which we needed to buy while we were away. Overall it was a very successful weekend and a great way to wear in our new seniors card, allowing us to catch up with family and friends and live a life comparable to when we were working, without the stress of worrying that we were eating into our retirement funds.

-----


Details of the Pensioner Excursion Ticket (PET):


The $2.50 Pensioner Excursion Ticket (PET) on CityRail services, on metropolitan and outer metropolitan local bus services and on government ferry services. It is also available on private regulated ferry services but restricted to local residents reliant on the ferry as their point of entry to the metropolitan and outer metropolitan public transport network, and requires prior registration with the local operator. From 1 July 2011 the PET has been available for use on light rail services. The PET is not sold on light rail and must be purchased before boarding. See the Buying Tickets page  for information


Country Pensioner Excursion tickets are designed to provide pensioners with an economical way to travel in regional and rural NSW and the ACT. At $2.50 for a one way, economy class trip, they are intended to help you get to neighbouring towns in order to visit friends and family, go shopping or keep medical, business or other appointments. Country Pensioner Excursion bookings may only be made within 7 days of travel.


As an added bonus, your Country Pensioner Excursion ticket will allow you to return to your place of origin provided you begin your return journey within 12 hours of arriving at your destination.


Country Pensioner Excursion tickets can't be bought or used for travel to or from locations or pass through the area bounded by and including Sydney, Nowra, Moss Vale, Lithgow, Muswellbrook, Dungog, Karuah and Booral. They also can't be bought or used for travel to Victoria or Queensland destinations.


This means these tickets can't be used to travel from the country to, or travel through, urban areas such as Sydney, Wollongong or Newcastle or to the Blue Mountains. If you wish to travel into the areas listed above you will need to buy a half-fare pensioner concession ticket or use a Pensioner Travel Voucher.


Who can use them?


If you hold one of the following concession cards, you are entitled to purchase Country Pensioner Excursion tickets:


- NSW, Victorian or ACT pensioner concession card

- NSW, Victorian or ACT war widow/er transport concession card

- Any Australian State or Territory issued Seniors Card, or

- Transport concession fare authority for former RailCorp and State Transit Authority employees.


You need to provide your concession card number when booking and collecting your ticket. Please remember to take the same concession card with you when you travel as our onboard staff will need to check it.

What is this Twitter / Instagram / Facebook thing and why should I get it?


Twitter, Instagram and Facebook, collectively belong to a group called Social Media. These are internet services which allow you to interact with friends, family and strangers online. While there is debate as to whether this is truly social or not, this article just aims to outline what each app, or program does, and why you may or may not like it.

 

Twitter


Twitter is about posting short messages of 140 characters that can be read by other people who are following you. It is used to share your thoughts on things that are happening, news events, or just your thoughts for the day. 

On Twitter, each short message is called a "tweet". Your followers are people who have chosen to be shown what tweets you post. Usually this starts as people you know, or often it comes from when you choose to follow someone else, they might follow you back. For example, you might start following @ForSeniors on Twitter, and then we will follow you back so we can see what you have to say.

One of the benefits of having Twitter is that it is a great way to make complaints against companies that will get noticed. Companies do not like having their faults aired publicly and often a complaining tweet will receive a very fast response, as they have programs to detect when their name is mentioned and then refer this to staff to deal with appropriately.  Twitter is also a nice easy step into social media, but most of the other social media we will talk about has the exact same capabilities and more.

To visit Twitter's website, click here

 

Instagram


Instagram is comparable to an online photo album, with the added bonus that both you and other people can comment on the album.  Instagram allows people to see little snapshots of your day and is a fun program that also keeps you in touch with what friends and family are doing. It is a simple app and is often installed on peoples phone, making it easy to view and upload (put pictures on the program so others can see them too) photos of what you are currently doing. This is more used by family and friends.

To visit Instagram's website, click here

  

Facebook


Facebook is similar to an interactive journal that you know is public. Facebook allows users to post pictures, comments, links and ideas on their own page and yours. It is a simple way to keep in touch with family and friends and see what they are up to.

Planning for retirement: 9 things you must consider before reaching retirement age

﻿

You may be considering retiring in the near future and looking forward to with a mixed sense of anticipation and dread. This article is a starting point to help you think about what you might want to research and know more about before you retire. 


1. Finance


Make sure you not only have enough money in your retirement fund, but you have a retirement savings plan as well.  There are many sources out there that will help you plan for retirement and explain your retirement pension, but you will need to find the one that is right for your situation. Ensure that you have the correct insurance for yourself and your properties. Retirement planning is an ongoing process and is important to consider before you reach retirement age.

The Australian government has a great resource, that can help you prepare http://australia.gov.au/topics/employment-and-workplace/retirement


2. Health 


- Have your health checked by your G.P. See if there is anything that you need to improve in your diet, or living habits. Ask your G.P to explain what routine health checks you will need to have and when. Ensure that you note these. Take full advantage of all the programs available for senior’s health. 


- Ensure that you have the correct health care and keep it up to date; the truth is that during retirement sometimes illness occurs and if this does happen, you want to be covered to the best of your ability. 


- Make improvements to your diet and exercise regime. You have the time now and no more excuses for poor living: improve your diet, ensure that you go on walks and start a healthy living regime. It is never too late and this is an easy way to start your retirement living and contribute to a healthy lifestyle. 


3. Social


- One of the most important things in retirement is to view retirement with a healthy positive outlook. There will be times where you miss the workplace, and many unexpected good and bad events can occur, but approaching retirement as a positive step and adventure, will make it much more enjoyable and decrease your stress levels. 


- Make sure you have thought of activities to become involved in, sitting at home and watching the television may sounds great, , but ensure you have social activities lined up to fight boredom and live a balanced life. 


- Find groups for activities you are interested in or you think you might be interested in and try them out. If you are shy or nervous, perhaps take a friend or partner along for the first time. 


- Create a social group. This may sound to many men like a feminine thing to do, but it is just as applicable to men.  Organize to meet monthly at a bar, or for dinner, If you have a shared hobby organsie an outing, or a shed to work in together to complete a program. 


- Go for your dreams. You may feel to old to start something new, but this is not true at all. Why not try something you have always wanted to do? If it is too strenuous then break it down into smaller chunks that are more manageable. 


- Make use of your wisdom. You may feel a sense of something missing once you leave the workplace, often this is to do with social interaction, or the respect and knowledge that you gained over the years at your job. As a retiree you still have this knowledge and these skills and they are invaluable. Share your knowledge and skills, you may wish to mentor young people in your field, or perhaps you are an able craftsman and would lie to share these skills, or help trouble youths learn them, or just come together with other men and women who have the same skills and share your knowledge. 


There are many volunteer positions that would love to have someone with your skills and knowledge some in and help. Many organsiations hire ex employees to help train new staff, or to help with side projects that the company does on a volunteer basis. Offer your services to schools, or volunteer organizations.


- Work only when you want to. If you have started you work again in retirement, then make sure you do fall back into the working routine, ensure that the work you take on allows you some time and freedom to focus on yourself and your family. 


- Keep mentally active. This does not mean sitting in front of the television: find or continue a hobby, seek out some adult learning classes, keep up with what is happening in the news, or find some activities that stimulate and challenge your brain. 


4. Maintain your fitness, or get active


One of the most important things to ensure that you do during retirement is to keep fit. Being physically active is so important, but finding the right sport can be difficult, as some require driving to a sports complex, while others are expensive and some are beyond what you can reasonably demand of your body.  You may also not relise how active you were in job. 

Why not try some sports out, after checking that they are ok with your doctor first. Sport should not make you so sore that you can no longer walk or move, a little bit of an ache here and there is ok, but you do not want ongoing aches or pains. Start off slowly and build up with exercise, and give your chosen sport a fair go, allow time for you to enjoy the sport and make friends. Some people say it takes at least 21 days to form a routine, but with a sport give it a few months before you don’t like it, unless it is causing you pain or discomfort. 

If you are busy and haven’t decided on how you want to keep fit, try light walking or swimming for the first little while of retirement, it is easy to fit into your regime and is free, or low cost, but make sure you are moving on a regular basis in retirement. 


5. What's available for seniors once they reach retirement age?


Make sure you know what is available in your area or city. There are great groups that always welcome newcomers, and that are doing great activities. Many of these groups may be doing activities that you had never even considered before or even heard of, but you may be surprised how much you enjoy them once you join up. Many of these groups are made up members who not only have an interest in the activity but also an interest in meeting new people from a range of backgrounds and walks of life. 


7. Wills


Ensure that your will is up to date. No further commentary needed!


8. Be informed


Don’t get scammed, and don’t be afraid to ask for help or clarification. The fact is many young people also get scammed, but many others narrowly avoid scams, as they have an informed social network and had they not had friends to tell them or warn them they too may have been scammed. The fact is there are many great deals out there, but there are also scams, if you think it is too good to be true ask around, research both on the internet and in traditional ways. If you are unsure about a particular website, contact us, and we will help you!


9. Don’t Fear technology 


The Internet and computers are great for retirees! Do not fear them, use them to your advantage. They can make the world smaller and easier to navigate and the best way to see the benefits is just to sit down and have a go. Just don’t fall into the trap of spending all day and night on the Internet, use it as a tool to improve your living and to bring the world closer, not to take you away from the world.

What is a seniors card and how do I get one?


A seniors card is a card issued by the various state governments in Australia to seniors citizens, which entitles them to reduced rates for some government services, as well as products and services in the private sector from participating businesses. Although seniors cards are issued separately in each state and territory, they can be used interstate for some products and services.


Getting a seniors card isn't as difficult as it sounds, and we have prepared a guide to show you how you can get a seniors card in Australia. We cover all of the states and territories in the guide, so you'll be covered no matter where in Australia you live.


The first Seniors Cards were issues in Western Australia in 1988 and it has a high recognition there, 86% of members there use their cards to get discounts. Other states aren't far behind, and the number of private sector businesses offering discounts to senior citizens is always increasing.

Social media etiquette tips for senior citizens

﻿One of the main things that seniors struggle with in social media apps, especially according to their kids and grandkids, is the unofficial etiquette rules which are normally followed by users. Use the simple concepts below to make sure that you are a valued Instagram user, and your kids and grandkids will be very impressed at how well you have picked things up:

  1. Don't comment on every photo that you see, comment occasionally.

  2. Make sure your comments are in context and are not cryptic, e.g. say "That's beautiful, it reminds me of that time in Cooma".

  3. Don't like every photo that you see, you can like more photos than you comment on, but don't over-do it. See your "likes" as a form of currency, use them more sparingly and people will value them more.

  4. Don't post too often, especially in a short period of time. A rule of thumb might be to post one or two photos a day as a maximum, and only if the photos are interesting. It is OK to make exceptions for special family events or holidays, use common sense - will people find this interesting?

  5. Instagram can and will be used as a verb, for example "I am going to Instagram a photo of this cow"

  6. "Gram" is not a way to short "Instagram", do not say "I just grammed that cow". Reversal: you can deliberately tease your kids or grandkids by doing this; watch their reactions. This is called "trolling".
